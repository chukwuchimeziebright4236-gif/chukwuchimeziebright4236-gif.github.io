Nigeria to Seek Compensation for Property Abandoned by Citizens Fleeing South Africa

The Nigerian Federal Government has officially launched diplomatic efforts to seek full financial compensation from South Africa for properties, businesses, and vehicles abandoned by Nigerian citizens fleeing the country.

The move follows a wave of nationwide anti-immigrant protests and rising xenophobic tensions in South Africa, which forced hundreds of Nigerians to enroll in a voluntary repatriation program to escape potential violence. Systematic Documentation and Diplomatic Pressure

Nigeria's Acting High Commissioner to South Africa, Ambassador Alexander Ajayi, confirmed the development, stating that the intervention will go far beyond just bringing citizens back home.

The Nigerian diplomatic mission has formally directed all returnees to compile a strict, verifiable inventory of their left-behind assets. These records will serve as the evidentiary foundation for future compensation claims presented to the South African government.

"This repatriation will not end with just taking people to Nigeria. We are going to systematically follow up on the information given to us. We will work with the South African government to get to the exact locations of all these businesses, shops, and properties and present them for possible compensation. We will not allow the labor people have suffered to build over the years to just go down the drain." — Ambassador Alexander Ajayi, Acting High Commissioner to South Africa

Key Details of the Crisis

  • The Triggers: Tensions boiled over following mass anti-immigration demonstrations organized by local movements demanding the deportation of foreign nationals, climaxing around a June 30 deadline. While many protests remained contained, isolated attacks and widespread fear sparked a massive exodus of foreign nationals, including Ghanaians and Zimbabweans.
  • The Evacuation: Hundreds of Nigerians have already safely arrived at the Murtala Muhammed International Airport in Lagos via chartered Air Peace flights coordinated by the Federal Government, with hundreds more currently awaiting repatriation.
  • The Resettlement: Upon arrival, the Nigerians in Diaspora Commission (NiDCOM) revealed that returnees are receiving immediate resettlement stipends and support packages from corporate donors and religious organizations to help them restart their lives.
  • The Documentation Trap: Addressing claims that the fleeing individuals were entirely undocumented, Ambassador Ajayi clarified that the majority of Nigerians entered South Africa legally. However, they were caught in severe, multi-year processing backlogs at the South African Department of Home Affairs, rendering them technically "undocumented" due to systemic delays in renewal applications.

While the South African government has deployed security forces to manage protest hotspots and prevent widespread looting, Pretoria has yet to issue a formal response regarding the impending compensation claims from Abuja. Diplomatic talks between the two African economic powerhouses are expected to intensify in the coming weeks.
