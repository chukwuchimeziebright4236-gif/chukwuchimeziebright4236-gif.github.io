Peace

This peace is completely distinct from the peace offered by this world system. In John 14:27, Jesus declared:

“Peace I leave with you; my peace I give unto you: not as the world giveth, give I unto you. Let not your heart be troubled, neither let it be afraid.”

To fully grasp the reality of Christ's peace, we must look to Isaiah 9:6, where He is prophetically titled the Prince of Peace. Understanding the depth of this divine peace requires a close examination of the legal benefits secured through His death and resurrection.

Reconciliation and Right Standing with God

A primary benefit of Christ's sacrifice is reconciliation. To reconcile means to restore an intimate friendship, brotherhood, or a father-son relationship after a deep, hostile separation. Due to original sin, mankind existed as spiritual enemies of God. However, the death and resurrection of Christ created the ultimate legal pathway for us to be reconciled back to the Father. Romans 5:10–11 establishes this truth:

“For if, when we were enemies, we were reconciled to God by the death of his Son, much more, being reconciled, we shall be saved by his life. And not only so, but we also joy in God through our Lord Jesus Christ, by whom we have now received the atonement.”

True peace materializes the moment you are reconciled and placed in right standing with God. Jesus, acting as the Prince of Peace, bridged the divide. Through Him, we are no longer outcasts; we are received as children and mature sons of God.

This reconciliation was necessary because sin entered the cosmos through one man, Adam, and death spread to all men because all sinned. Before the Mosaic law was given, sin was already present in the earth, though it is not legally imputed when there is no law. Nevertheless, spiritual and physical death reigned supreme from Adam to Moses, even over those whose transgressions did not mimic the exact rebellion of Adam, who was a prophetic pattern of the One to come (Romans 5:12–14).

The Law of Sin, Death, and the Realms of Custody

The unbending spiritual consequence of sin is death. Sin functions as a definitive death sentence because God exalts His spoken Word even above His own name. Consequently, when Adam succumbed to sin, the entirety of mankind fell under that judicial sentence.

We see a parallel in modern human civilization. Human legislators establish penal codes, some of which carry capital punishment. When a criminal is convicted of a capital crime under due process, their physical body is executed. However, human beings consist of both an immaterial spirit and a physical body. When physical execution occurs, only the body dies; the individual continues to exist.

In the case of an unrepentant sinner, their human spirit descends immediately into Hades—the subterranean place of the dead, characterized by fire and carnal confinement. Hades functions as a maximum-security spiritual holding facility. Following His crucifixion, Jesus descended into this very realm because He was bearing the collective weight of the world's sins, using that opportunity to preach to the spirits in prison (1 Holy Peter 3:19).

This temporary confinement in Hades can be understood as an extension of the first death. However, the scriptures warn of a final, permanent second death. Following the Great White Throne Judgment, anyone whose name is not found written in the Book of Life will be cast directly into the Lake of Fire (Revelation 20:14–15).

This operates exactly like our modern legal system. Security operatives arrest a suspect and hold them in temporary custody. From there, the individual is formally arraigned before a court of law. Once the judicial verdict is delivered based on established statutory guidelines, the prisoner is transferred from temporary custody to face permanent execution.

Therefore, when the Scripture promises that whosoever believes in Jesus shall not perish but have everlasting life, it means they are legally exempt from entering the prison of Hades, and completely insulated from the second death in the Lake of Fire. This is the structural mechanism God designed to execute justice upon disobedience.

Because believers are reconciled to God through the death of His Son, they are saved from the impending wrath of God. Divine wrath no longer rests upon those who accept that Jesus died for them and that God raised Him from the dead. Absolute peace now exists between the Creator and the believer because God receives them into His family. Jesus Christ stands as the firstborn from the dead, and we follow in His direct lineage as sons of God.

The Mandate of Peacemakers and the Sovereignty of Mercy

According to Isaiah 9:7, of the increase of Christ’s government and peace, there shall be no end. In Matthew 5:9, Jesus stated: “Blessed are the peacemakers: for they shall be called the children of God.”

Within this context, true peacemaking is the active deployment of the ministry of reconciliation—bringing lost sinners out of hostility and into structural alignment with God. A sinner living apart from Christ remains legally positioned under divine wrath. Divine peace is therefore an elite spiritual asset, far more valuable than temporary material riches, because it outlasts this world system and endures throughout eternity.

Our enjoyment of these spiritual assets relies entirely upon God's active mercy and grace. Mercy, at its core, is the extension of divine forgiveness. When Zechariah, the father of John the Baptist, was filled with the Holy Spirit at his son’s birth, he prophesied concerning the child’s destiny in Luke 1:76–77:

“And thou, child, shalt be called the prophet of the Highest: for thou shalt go before the face of the Lord to prepare his ways; To give knowledge of salvation unto his people by the remission of their sins, Through the tender mercy of our God...”

It is exclusively His tender mercy that qualifies human beings for salvation; it is not a product of human merit.

Paul details the sovereign nature of this choice in Romans 9. He notes that while natural Israel possessed the adoption, the glory, the covenants, the giving of the law, the temple service, and the prophetic promises, not all who descend from natural Israel belong to the spiritual lineage of promise. The covenant line was strictly designated: “In Isaac shall thy seed be called.”

This was further demonstrated when Rebecca conceived twins by our father Isaac. Before Jacob and Esau were born, and before they had done any good or evil—insuring that God’s purpose according to election might stand independently of human works—it was declared: “The elder shall serve the younger.” As it is written: “Jacob have I loved, but Esau have I hated.”

This prompts a vital question: Is God unrighteous or unjust? Absolutely not. He declared plainly to Moses: “I will have mercy on whom I will have mercy, and I will have compassion on whom I will have compassion.” Therefore, salvation and spiritual elevation do not depend on human wishing or carnal strivings, but entirely on God who shows mercy.

The Scripture records a similar principle regarding Pharaoh: “Even for this same purpose have I raised thee up, that I might show my power in thee, and that my name might be declared throughout all the earth.” God extends mercy according to His sovereign will, and allows others to remain hardened in their rebellion.

Consequently, Paul concludes in Romans 9:30–32 that the Gentiles, who were not actively pursuing righteousness, apprehended it—even the righteousness which is by faith. Meanwhile, natural Israel, who pursued the law of righteousness, failed to attain it. Why? Because they sought it through legalistic works rather than through the mechanism of faith. God shifted the operational matrix from legalistic performance to divine grace. Righteousness is now accessed simply by believing.

The Operational Fusion of Grace, Favour, and Peace

The historical Abrahamic covenant provided immense blessing to his descendants. The only way to actively participate in that blessing is through the alignment of righteousness. One of the primary reasons Jesus endured the cross was to extend the blessing of Abraham beyond natural Israel, allowing the Gentile nations to fully inherit it. We were chosen by a distinct act of mercy, justified through the righteousness that comes by faith—believing in our hearts that God raised Jesus from the dead and confessing with our mouths that He is Lord.

This kingdom system functions by grace, translated from the Greek word *Charis*, which explicitly means the unearned, free gift of God. When an individual is positioned in righteousness, God blankets them with favor. However, before blessing and favor manifest externally, mercy and grace must establish the foundation. As Romans 8:30 outlines the divine progression:

“Moreover whom he did predestinate, them he also called: and whom he called, them he also justified: and whom he justified, them he also glorified.”

To be glorified means to be structurally invested with divine wisdom, riches, honor, power, and majesty. These components are the natural byproducts of mercy and righteousness working within your daily walk with God.

Peace functions as a highly effective spiritual weapon to reconcile humanity to God. Our corporate assignment as believers mirrors the mandate given to John the Baptist. We are called to go before the face of the Lord to prepare His ways, removing spiritual obstacles and turning backsliders and sinners alike to make absolute peace with God.

The peace of God, which passes all intellectual understanding, acts as an internal guard for your heart and mind. Conversely, there are three distinct indicators that verify a structural leak or loss of this peace:

  1. Worry: Chronic anxiety regarding temporal concerns.
  2. Anger: Uncontrolled emotional volatility and strife.
  3. Fear: A state of terror that paralyzes faith.

To combat these vulnerabilities, Ephesians 6:15 commands us to have our “feet shod with the preparation of the gospel of peace.” The gospel is, by definition, good news. This explains why Jesus instructed His disciples to go into all the world and preach the gospel to every creature. Having your feet shod with this preparation means that wherever you step, you convert that environment through godly character, sound speech, and precise obedience to the direction of the Holy Spirit.
