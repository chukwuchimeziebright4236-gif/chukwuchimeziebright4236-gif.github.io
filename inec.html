INEC Yet to Receive Certified True Copy of Court Judgment Voiding Nigeria Democratic Congress Registration

Electoral commission maintains standard administrative protocols await official legal documentation following Federal High Court ruling in Lokoja.
By Chukwuma Akhimien | Political Correspondent

The Independent National Electoral Commission (INEC) has stated that it is yet to receive the Certified True Copy (CTC) of the Federal High Court judgment sitting in Lokoja, Kogi State, which set aside an earlier ruling compelling the commission to register the Nigeria Democratic Congress (NDC) as a legally recognized political party.

Reliable sources within the commission's legal department confirmed over the weekend that while the electoral umpire is fully aware of the media reports regarding the judicial development, standard operational and administrative procedures mandate that no formal step or enforcement can occur until the official certified paperwork is processed and served on the commission headquarters.

Legal Thresholds
The Federal High Court in Lokoja nullified its previous judgment on the grounds of fresh legal arguments indicating that the party's promoters had bypassed mandatory constitutional and regulatory administrative processes required by the electoral body for political party recognition.

The legal controversy dates back to a previous judicial directive that had ordered INEC to include the Nigeria Democratic Congress on the national roster of registered political parties. That decision had sparked immediate criticism from competing political associations and legal analysts, who claimed that allowing groups to circumvent formal verification procedures undermines the institutional integrity of the electoral guidelines ahead of future election cycles.

In response to the latest ruling setting aside that order, leaders of the NDC have expressed strong dissatisfaction, declaring their intention to approach the Court of Appeal to contest the decision. Legal representatives for the political group argue that they had met all reasonable constitutional obligations and that the sudden reversal creates an unfavorable precedent for democratic pluralism in the country.

Meanwhile, civil society organizations focused on electoral reforms have urged INEC to maintain transparency. Analysts point out that strict compliance with official certified copies of judgements prevents administrative confusion and ensures that political entities are treated according to uniform rules established under the Electoral Act.

Officials familiar with the registration tracking desks noted that as soon as the certified document is received from the Lokoja court registry, INEC’s legal team will review the fine details to issue a decisive regulatory update regarding the party’s current standing.
