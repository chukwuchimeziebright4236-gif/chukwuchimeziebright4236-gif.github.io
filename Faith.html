Faith

Faith was defined by Paul the apostle as the “substance of things hoped for, the evidence of things not seen” (Hebrews 11:1). In structural terms, a substance is the raw material out of which a final product is created. For instance, cassava is the substance used to process garri, and sugarcane is the substance used to manufacture sugar.

If we assume that garri is what we are hoping for, and we possess cassava, we hold the essential raw material; all that remains is to process it into reality. However, the substance referred to in scripture is not physical; it is a spiritual substance. It exists in the realm of the spirit, and faith is the spiritual conduit that transports it into our material world where it becomes a tangible reality. In Jeremiah 29:11, God declares:

“For I know the thoughts that I think toward you, saith the Lord, thoughts of peace, and not of evil, to give you an expected end.”

God intentionally provides us with structural reference points to hope for. He does this through direct prophecies, dreams, spiritual visions, and the written scriptures. The reality is that there is no other mechanism through which you can access the future and hope God has engineered for you except through faith. God’s plans for your life exist fundamentally as spiritual substances.

Consider God's covenant with Abraham: God commanded him to depart to a land He would reveal, promising to transform him into a great nation, magnify his name, and make him a blessing. God also declared, “I will bless them that bless thee, and curse him that curseth thee.” When these declarations were made, they had not yet manifested physically; they existed purely in the realm of the spirit. This is precisely what Paul meant when he wrote that faith is the substance of things hoped for.

The Evidence of Things Not Seen

The second operational component of faith is that it acts as the “evidence of things not seen.” Throughout Abraham's journey with God, he was commanded to travel to an unseen land based entirely on divine promises. In this dynamic, God Himself acts as the ultimate evidence or absolute witness of the unseen reality.

To use an earthly analogy: if I instruct you to travel to a specific location with the promise of a financial gift, your immediate consideration would be my personal status. You would evaluate whether I possess the capacity to execute the promise and whether my character is trustworthy. Armed with that structural knowledge, you can journey confidently because my track record functions as your evidence.

This underlines why it is critically essential to know God intimately. Exploits within the Kingdom of God are not a general occurrence for everyone; they are reserved exclusively for those who possess an intimate knowledge of God. Hebrews 11:6 reinforces this legal requirement:

“But without faith it is impossible to please him: for he that commeth to God must believe that he is, and that he is a rewarder of them that diligently seek him.”

The individuals who manifested extraordinary faith throughout biblical history did so as a direct result of their knowledge of God. As Hebrews 11:11 records: “Through faith also Sara herself received strength to conceive seed, and was delivered of a child when she was past age, because she judged him faithful who had promised.” Sarah’s faith was anchored in the direct knowledge that God is completely trustworthy.

Similarly, Noah was accounted as a righteous man because he maintained a deep, reverent fear of the Lord. When God warned him regarding a catastrophic global flood—an event completely unprecedented in human history—Noah took God’s word with absolute seriousness and constructed an ark in precise accordance with divine instructions.

The Dimension of Spiritual Understanding

Beyond the dimensions of personal knowledge and reverent fear, there is another operational facet of faith that relies entirely upon your level of spiritual understanding. This involves how accurately you comprehend God's written Word, His immediate prophecies, and the dreams He communicates regarding your life. An individual can fear God and know Him deeply enough to produce basic results, yet fail simply because they do not understand what God has spoken to them.

In Genesis 13:14–15, God commanded Abram (whose name was later changed to Abraham): “Lift up now thine eyes, and look from the place where thou art northward, and southward, and eastward, and westward: For all the land which thou seest, to thee will I give it, and to thy seed forever.” The operational focus here is on the command to see and God’s promise to make his descendants as numerous as the dust of the earth.

When God conveyed these terms, Abraham was facing an apparent physical impossibility. In Genesis 15:2–3, Abraham responded: “Lord God, what wilt thou give me, seeing I go childless, and the steward of my house is this Eliezer of Damascus? Behold, to me thou hast given no seed: and, lo, one born in my house is mine heir.”

In response, the Word of the Lord corrected his perspective, declaring that Eliezer would not be his heir. God brought Abraham outside his tent into the starlight and commanded him: “Look now toward heaven, and tell the stars, if thou be able to number them: and he said unto him, So shall thy seed be.” The scripture records that Abraham believed the Lord, and it was accounted to him as righteousness. Abraham’s faith materialized because he shifted into a clear structural understanding of what God was illustrating.

True faith requires corresponding action to be structurally effective. It cannot stop at a passive, mental belief where people merely confess, “We believe God is capable.” While that confession is fundamentally good, it remains ineffective when standing face-to-face with a critical crisis. To fully manifest biblical faith, you must fuse all three dimensions together:

  • The absolute, reverent fear of God.
  • The intimate, personal knowledge of God’s nature.
  • A clear spiritual understanding of His ways and His direct words to you.

The Shield of Faith as an Armor of Defense

Having established these foundational pillars, we must recognize faith as a strategic defensive weapon. Ephesians 6:16 commands believers to deploy this asset within the armor of God:

“Above all, taking the shield of faith, wherewith ye shall be able to quench all the fiery darts of the wicked.”

David serves as an excellent historical example of an individual who wielded faith as a defensive weapon to protect the ranks of Israel. Goliath of Gath stood and issued a continuous challenge to the armies of Israel, demanding they select a single champion to fight him in single combat. The terms were absolute: if the Israelite champion killed Goliath, the Philistines would serve Israel; if Goliath prevailed, Israel would enter into complete servitude. For forty consecutive days, morning and evening, Goliath stood and openly defied the ranks of Israel.

During this period, Jesse sent David to deliver provisions to his elder brothers who were serving under King Saul in the army. Arriving at the perimeter, David left his supplies with the baggage keeper and ran directly to the battle line to greet his brothers. As they conversed, Goliath stepped forward from the Philistine ranks and repeated his defiant challenge. David listened closely as the men of Israel fled from the giant in absolute terror.

The soldiers informed David that the king would reward anyone who killed the Philistine with immense wealth, marry his daughter to him, and grant his father’s house total tax exemption in Israel. David turned to the men standing near him and inquired: “What shall be done to the man that killeth this Philistine, and taketh away the reproach from Israel? for who is this uncircumcised Philistine, that he should defy the armies of the living God?”

David’s words were quickly reported to King Saul, who immediately summoned him. David told the king: “Let no man's heart fail because of him; thy servant will go and fight with this Philistine.” Saul objected, stating that David was merely an adolescent while Goliath had been a trained operative of war since his youth.

David answered by detailing his operational track record: “Thy servant kept his father's sheep, and there came a lion, and a bear, and took a lamb out of the flock: And I went out after him, and smote him, and delivered it out of his mouth: and when he arose against me, I caught him by his beard, and smote him, and slew him. Thy servant slew both the lion and the bear: and this uncircumcised Philistine shall be as one of them, seeing he hath defied the armies of the living God.” David added confidently:

“The Lord that delivered me out of the paw of the lion, and out of the paw of the bear, he will deliver me out of the hand of this Philistine.”

Convinced, Saul told him, “Go, and the Lord be with you.” Refusing the king’s heavy armor, David took his staff, selected five smooth stones from the brook, placed them inside his shepherd's pouch, and advanced toward the Philistine with his sling in hand.

When Goliath looked closely and saw David, he disdained him because of his youth and ruddy appearance. The Philistine shouted: “Am I a dog, that thou comest to me with staves?” Goliath cursed David by his gods, saying, “Come to me, and I will give thy flesh unto the fowls of the air, and to the beasts of the field.”

David countered with a declaration of faith-backed warfare:

“Thou comest to me with a sword, and with a spear, and with a shield: but I come to thee in the name of the Lord of hosts, the God of the armies of Israel, whom thou hast defied. This day will the Lord deliver thee into mine hand; and I will smite thee, and take thine head from thee... that all the earth may know that there is a God in Israel. And all this assembly shall know that the Lord saveth not with sword and spear: for the battle is the Lord's, and he will give you into our hands.”

As the Philistine advanced, David ran swiftly toward the battle line. He reached into his bag, drew out a stone, and slung it with precision. The stone struck the Philistine, sinking deeply into his forehead, causing him to fall face down upon the earth. David prevailed over the champion using only a sling and a stone. Because there was no sword in his hand, David ran, stood over the fallen giant, drew Goliath’s own sword from its sheath, and decapitated him. Seeing their champion destroyed, the Philistine army broke formation and fled in panic.

Living by the Divine Standard

It is impossible to navigate this life successfully without faith; it functions as our primary shield of defense. In ancient warfare, long before the advent of modern firearms, soldiers relied heavily on catapults, swords, helmets, breastplates, bows, and physical shields to quench flaming arrows. Scripture purposefully mirrors this imagery, identifying faith as that protective shield.

Operating in faith means that when you know God and comprehend His Word, you execute corresponding actions based on that Word, entirely independent of the surrounding crisis or physical conditions. This is why the scripture emphasizes that the just shall live by faith. If your actions are governed strictly by physical circumstances, you are not operating in faith.

God maintains an unyielding structural standard and a sovereign way of doing things, regardless of how corrupt or wicked the world becomes. He has not ceased providing for the poor, instructing the humble, or executing absolute justice for the oppressed. Furthermore, His spiritual laws remain absolute: anyone who rejects the substitutionary sacrifice of Jesus Christ will perish apart from Him. God is entirely unbiased, no respecter of persons, and He executes what is perfectly right regardless of human trends.
